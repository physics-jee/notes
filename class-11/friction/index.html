Friction

A force that opposes relative motion between surfaces in contact.

1. Nature of Friction

Friction is a contact force that resists relative motion or the tendency of relative motion between two surfaces in contact.

  • Acts tangentially at the surface of contact
  • Opposes motion or impending motion
Friction is not a “reaction” force — it is an adaptive force.

2. Types of Friction

  • Static Friction — prevents relative motion
  • Kinetic (Sliding) Friction — acts during motion
  • Rolling Friction — much smaller than sliding friction

3. Static Friction

Static friction adjusts its magnitude to match the applied force, up to a maximum value.

\[ f_s \le f_{s,\max} = \mu_s N \]
  • \(N\) is the normal reaction
  • \(\mu_s\) is the coefficient of static friction
Static friction is whatever it needs to be — until it can’t.

4. Kinetic Friction

Once relative motion begins, friction becomes approximately constant.

\[ f_k = \mu_k N \]
  • \(\mu_k < \mu_s\)
  • Direction always opposite to relative velocity
Kinetic friction depends weakly on speed at the JEE level.

5. Direction of Friction

The direction of friction is determined by the tendency of relative motion, not by the applied force.

  • Ask: “If friction were absent, how would the body move?”
  • Friction acts opposite to that tendency
Direction errors in friction problems destroy otherwise correct solutions.

6. Limiting Friction & Angle of Friction

\[ \tan \theta = \mu_s \]

Here, \(\theta\) is the angle of friction, the angle between the resultant reaction and the normal.

7. Common Misconceptions

  • Friction ≠ always \(\mu N\) (only true at limiting or kinetic case)
  • Normal reaction is not always equal to weight
  • Action–reaction pairs never act on the same body
Most friction problems are solved by thinking before calculating.
