Motion in One Dimension

The first complete mathematical treatment of change.

What Is This Chapter About?

This chapter is about motion — a naturally occurring phenomenon — something we observe every single day. In physics, the study of motion is called Mechanics. Motion is defined as change of position with time, as measured by an observer.

This chapter is mathematically intensive. The physical quantity Position is expressed as a function of another physical quantity, Time. Nearly everything learned in Functions and Calculus is now applied as a tool to describe physical change.

Why Is This Chapter Sequenced Here?

This is the first chapter in any general course on Mechanics because:

  • Quantitative Training: It teaches how to describe motion using numbers and units instead of vague words.
  • The Calculus Playground: It allows differentiation and integration to be applied to physical reality without the distraction of complex geometry.
  • Vector "Lite": Motion along a straight line postpones the full load of 2D/3D vectors. In one dimension, a simple plus or minus sign carries directional information.
  • Description vs. Cause: We focus strictly on what is happening, not why. The relation \( x = f(t) \) does not mean that time causes position. It expresses functional dependence, not causation.

Mathematical description comes first. The study of causes (forces) comes later.

Why Mechanics Is Studied First

Everything in the physical world exists within the structural framework of Space and Time. Physics studies natural phenomena as changes of state, and those changes occur within this framework. For that reason, mechanics forms the foundation for every other branch of physics.

The way we quantify change here becomes a universal language. Whether studying heat, light, electromagnetism or atoms later, the same structural logic of change will apply.

In general, change in any physical quantity (PQ) with respect to time is defined as:

\[ \Delta (PQ) = (PQ)_f - (PQ)_i \]

where \( (PQ)_f \) and \( (PQ)_i \) are the values of the quantity at \( t = t_f \) and \( t = t_i \), respectively.

When a quantity varies with position instead of time, we write:

\[ \Delta (PQ) = (PQ)_2 - (PQ)_1 \]

Here we avoid using the words "initial" and "final" because time flows in one direction, whereas spatial points do not carry an inherent direction.

How It Should Be Studied

  • Pick a Side: Choose a coordinate system intentionally and remain consistent.
  • Respect the Sign: In one dimension, the sign (\( + \) or \( - \)) is the direction. Treat it as physical information, not merely a mathematical symbol.
  • Graphs are Geometry: A curve is not just a picture. Its slope and area carry physical meaning. Learn to interpret graphs geometrically.
  • Ditch the Shortcuts: Understand derivations as a source of formulas. Memorization without understanding, fails when faced with unfamiliar problems.
