Motion in One Dimension

Describing how objects move along a straight line.

1. Frame of Reference & Motion

Motion must be described relative to a chosen reference point and a chosen direction.

Motion is not absolute — it depends on *where* and *how* you measure it.

2. Distance vs. Displacement

Distance is the total path length covered (scalar), whereas displacement is the net change in position (vector).

  • Distance ≥ |Displacement|
  • Displacement can be positive, negative, or zero

3. Speed & Velocity

Speed is how fast an object moves (scalar). Velocity is the rate of change of displacement (vector).

Average Velocity: \( \bar v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \)
Average Speed: \( \text{speed} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}} \)
Instantaneous velocity is the value of velocity at a specific moment in time.

4. Acceleration

Acceleration measures how quickly velocity changes.

Average Acceleration: \[ \bar a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]
Uniform acceleration means the rate of change of velocity is constant.

5. Kinematic Equations (Constant Acceleration)

When acceleration is constant, the following relations connect velocity, displacement, and time:

\( v = u + at \) \( \quad s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \) \( \quad v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)

Here, \(u\) is initial velocity, \(v\) is final velocity, \(a\) is constant acceleration, and \(s\) is displacement. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

6. Motion Graphs

Graphs give visual insight into motion:

  • Position–Time (x–t): Slope gives velocity
  • Velocity–Time (v–t): Slope gives acceleration; area under curve gives displacement
Recognizing shapes — horizontal lines, straight slopes, curves — is often more useful than memorizing equations.
