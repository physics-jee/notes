Motion in One Dimension

Why physics begins with motion.

Why Do We Begin Physics with Mechanics?

Physics seeks to understand the natural world through observation, measurement, and logical laws. When we examine nature at its most immediate and accessible level, one phenomenon stands out as the most common, most directly experienced, and most universally observed: motion.

Motion is an inseparable part of everyday human experience. We walk, run, and ride bicycles. Even during rest or sleep, motion does not cease: air continuously moves into and out of our lungs, and blood flows through arteries and veins.

Motion is not confined to objects we can see and touch. The Earth rotates about its axis and revolves around the Sun. The Sun itself moves through the Milky Way galaxy. At every observable scale of the universe, motion is present.

Because motion is so fundamental and so pervasive, understanding it becomes the first logical step in understanding the universe itself. Mechanics provides the basic language and laws required to describe motion quantitatively.

Physics begins with mechanics not by tradition, but by necessity.

What Is Motion?

Motion is defined as the phenomenon in which the position of an object changes with time.

An object is said to be in a state of motion if it continuously changes its position with respect to its surroundings as time passes. Conversely, an object is said to be at rest if its position does not change with respect to its surroundings.

However, rest and motion are not absolute concepts. They are relative.

Rest and motion are properties of the object and the observer together.

Motion as the Study of Space and Time

At its most fundamental level, the study of motion is the study of space and time.

For anything to exist meaningfully in physics, two questions must be answerable:

Where does it exist?    and    When does it exist?

Motion arises naturally when the position of an object in space changes with time. Thus, motion is not an extra feature imposed on nature—it is a direct consequence of describing existence using space and time.

Kinematics begins by assigning positions as functions of time.
