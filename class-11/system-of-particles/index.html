System of Particles

Collective motion, momentum, and interactions.

1. What Is a System of Particles?

A system of particles is a collection of two or more particles considered together for analysis.

  • Internal forces → act between particles of the system
  • External forces → act on the system from outside
Internal forces cannot change the total momentum of a system.

2. Centre of Mass

The centre of mass (COM) is the point that moves as if the entire mass of the system were concentrated there.

For discrete particles: \[ \vec R = \frac{\sum m_i \vec r_i}{\sum m_i} \]
The centre of mass is a mathematical construct, not necessarily a physical point.

3. Motion of the Centre of Mass

The acceleration of the centre of mass depends only on external forces.

\[ M\vec a_{\text{CM}} = \vec F_{\text{external}} \]
  • Internal forces cancel in pairs
  • COM follows Newton’s second law
A system behaves like a single particle when viewed from the COM perspective.

4. Linear Momentum of a System

Total momentum of a system is the vector sum of momenta of all particles.

\[ \vec P = \sum m_i \vec v_i = M \vec v_{\text{CM}} \]

5. Conservation of Linear Momentum

If the net external force on a system is zero, total linear momentum remains constant.

\[ \vec P_{\text{initial}} = \vec P_{\text{final}} \]
Momentum conservation is more fundamental than energy conservation.

6. Collisions

A collision is a short-duration interaction involving large internal forces.

  • Momentum is conserved (if external forces negligible)
  • Kinetic energy may or may not be conserved

7. Types of Collisions

Elastic Collision
  • Momentum conserved
  • Kinetic energy conserved
Inelastic Collision
  • Momentum conserved
  • Kinetic energy not conserved

In a perfectly inelastic collision, particles stick together after collision.

8. Coefficient of Restitution

The coefficient of restitution \(e\) measures the elasticity of a collision.

\[ e = \frac{\text{relative speed of separation}} {\text{relative speed of approach}} \]
  • \(0 \le e \le 1\)
  • \(e = 1\): elastic
  • \(e = 0\): perfectly inelastic
Restitution connects kinematics with momentum conservation.

9. Common Mistakes

  • Applying energy conservation to inelastic collisions
  • Ignoring external impulses
  • Confusing COM velocity with particle velocity
