Elasticity

How materials respond to applied forces.

1. Deformation

Deformation is the change in shape or size of a body under the action of external forces.

  • Change in length
  • Change in volume
  • Change in shape
Deformation is a geometric change; elasticity is a material property.

2. Elasticity

Elasticity is the ability of a material to regain its original shape and size after removal of deforming forces.

  • Perfectly elastic bodies are idealizations
  • Real materials have elastic limits

3. Stress and Strain

Stress
  • Internal restoring force per unit area
  • Cause of deformation
Strain
  • Fractional change in dimension
  • Effect of stress
Normal Stress: \[ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} \]

Longitudinal Strain: \[ \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L} \]
Stress has units; strain does not.

4. Hooke’s Law

Within elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain.

\[ \text{Stress} \propto \text{Strain} \]
Hooke’s law is a linear approximation, not a universal truth.

5. Elastic Moduli

Young’s Modulus

\[ Y = \frac{\text{longitudinal stress}}{\text{longitudinal strain}} \]

Bulk Modulus

\[ K = \frac{\text{volume stress}}{\text{volume strain}} \]

Shear Modulus

\[ G = \frac{\text{shear stress}}{\text{shear strain}} \]
Higher modulus → greater stiffness, not greater strength.

6. Poisson’s Ratio

When a body is stretched, it contracts laterally.

\[ \nu = -\frac{\text{lateral strain}}{\text{longitudinal strain}} \]
  • Dimensionless quantity
  • Typical values: 0 to 0.5

7. Elastic Potential Energy

Energy stored in a body due to elastic deformation.

For a spring: \[ U = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \]
Elastic energy is recoverable energy.

8. Stress–Strain Curve

  • Proportional limit
  • Elastic limit
  • Yield point
  • Breaking point
Ductility and brittleness are read from the stress–strain curve.

9. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing elasticity with strength
  • Applying Hooke’s law beyond elastic limit
  • Forgetting sign convention in Poisson’s ratio
