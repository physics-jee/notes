Science: Method and Knowledge

1. Science as Method and Knowledge

Science has two inseparable meanings.

  • Science as a methodology — a disciplined way of investigating the physical world.
  • Science as a body of knowledge — the organized collection of facts, laws, and theories obtained through that methodology.

The body of knowledge is valid only because it has been built using the method.

Science is not just what we know. It is how we know it.

2. Reproducibility and Controlled Experiment

A scientific claim becomes reliable only if it can be independently reproduced.

Reproducibility requires:

  • Clearly defined experimental setup
  • Controlled environmental conditions
  • Standardized measurement procedures
  • Explicitly stated assumptions

Without control of conditions, results cannot be verified.

Scientific knowledge is conditional upon well-defined experimental circumstances.

3. Scientific Laws and Their Domains

A scientific law expresses a consistent quantitative relation between measurable quantities.

However, every law has a domain of validity.

Examples of Domain Conditions

  • Newton’s Laws apply in inertial frames and at speeds much less than the speed of light.
  • Hooke’s Law is valid only within the elastic limit of a material.
  • Coulomb’s Law strictly applies to point charges or spherically symmetric charge distributions.
  • Ohm’s Law holds only when temperature and physical properties of the conductor remain constant.
A scientific law is always conditional. Its validity depends on specified assumptions.

4. Refinement and Extension

Scientific knowledge is not absolute. It is progressively refined.

Classical mechanics works within a wide range of conditions. Relativity and quantum mechanics extend its applicability to extreme regimes.

New theories expand domains; they do not arbitrarily discard verified results.

5. Why This Matters for Physics

Physics relies heavily on precise measurement and clearly defined conditions.

Its laws are powerful because:

  • They are experimentally verified
  • They are mathematically expressed
  • They specify their domains of validity
The reliability of physics comes from reproducibility, precision, and clearly stated limits.
