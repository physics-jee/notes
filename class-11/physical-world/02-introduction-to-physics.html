Introduction to Physics

1. Branches of Knowledge

Human knowledge can broadly be divided into:

  • Formal Sciences
  • Natural Sciences

Formal Sciences

These include mathematics and logic. They study abstract structures and relations. Their truths follow from definitions and deduction.

Natural Sciences

These study the physical world. Their conclusions must be verified by observation and experiment.

Major branches of natural science include:

  • Physics
  • Chemistry
  • Biology
  • Astronomy
Formal sciences deal with abstract consistency. Natural sciences deal with physical reality.

2. Physics as a Branch of Natural Science

Physics is the most fundamental branch of natural science.

It studies the basic constituents of the physical world:

  • Matter
  • Energy
  • Space
  • Time

Other sciences depend on these foundations. Chemistry studies interactions of matter. Biology studies organized living systems. Astronomy studies large-scale structures. All rely on physical laws.

3. Physics as a Quantitative Discipline

Physics does not merely describe phenomena. It quantifies them.

It seeks mathematical relations between measurable quantities.

For example, instead of saying “objects fall,” physics states:

\( F = ma \)

It relates force, mass, and acceleration through a precise equation.

Physics studies relations, not isolated facts.

4. Change and Invariance

The physical world is a world of change. Objects move, temperatures vary, systems evolve.

Physics studies:

  • How quantities change
  • What causes those changes
  • What remains invariant during change

Conservation laws — such as conservation of energy — reveal what remains constant even as systems evolve.

5. The Role of Measurement

Physics depends on meticulous and reproducible measurement.

Every physical law connects measurable quantities. Without measurement, there is no verification.

Precision is not decoration. It is essential.

Physics is the disciplined attempt to describe matter and energy in space and time through rigorous quantitative relations.
