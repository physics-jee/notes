Physical Laws and Theories

1. Organization of Scientific Knowledge

Scientific knowledge is not a collection of isolated facts. It is structured.

Through repeated experiment and reproducibility, regular patterns are identified. These patterns are expressed as laws.

Collections of related laws are then organized and explained within broader theoretical frameworks.

Science progresses from observation → law → theory.

2. Physical Laws

A physical law is a concise mathematical relation between measurable quantities.

It summarizes a consistent experimental pattern.

Examples:

  • Newton’s Second Law: \( F = ma \)
  • Hooke’s Law: \( F = -kx \) (within elastic limit)
  • Boyle’s Law: \( PV = \text{constant} \)
  • Coulomb’s Law: \( F \propto \frac{1}{r^2} \)

Each law holds within specific experimental conditions.

A law describes a regularity observed under defined conditions.

3. Principles

Some statements in physics are broader than individual laws. These are often called principles.

Examples include:

  • Conservation of Energy
  • Conservation of Momentum
  • Conservation of Charge

These principles have been found to hold across extremely wide domains.

Unlike specific empirical laws, they are not tied to narrow experimental setups. They represent structural invariances of nature.

Principles express what remains unchanged across diverse physical processes.

4. Scientific Theories

A scientific theory is a coherent framework that organizes and explains a set of laws.

It identifies the underlying assumptions and fundamental postulates of a branch of physics.

Examples:

  • The Kinetic Theory of Gases explains the gas laws using molecular motion.
  • Classical Electrodynamics leads to the wave description of light.
  • Quantum Theory organizes atomic-scale phenomena.

A theory selects certain laws as fundamental and derives others from them.

A theory is not a stronger law. It is an explanatory structure that organizes laws.

5. Hierarchy of Structure

Scientific knowledge can therefore be viewed hierarchically:

  • Experimental observations
  • Empirical laws
  • Broad principles
  • Organizing theories

Laws summarize patterns. Principles identify invariance. Theories provide structure.

Together, they form the architecture of science.
