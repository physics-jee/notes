Physical Laws and Theories

Understanding how physics is structurally organized.

1. What is a Physical Law?

A physical law is a concise mathematical relation between measurable quantities. It summarizes a consistent and reproducible pattern observed in nature.

  • Newton’s Second Law: \( F = ma \)
  • Hooke’s Law: \( F = -kx \) (within elastic limit)
  • Boyle’s Law: \( PV = \text{constant} \)
  • Coulomb’s Law: \( F \propto \frac{1}{r^2} \)

A law does not explain why a phenomenon occurs. It states how measurable quantities are related under defined conditions. Its validity always depends on its domain of applicability.

A law summarizes an experimentally observed regularity in mathematical form.

2. What is a Scientific Theory?

A scientific theory is a coherent framework that organizes multiple laws under a common set of assumptions and fundamental principles.

A theory explains laws by identifying deeper structural relations.

  • Kinetic Theory explains gas laws using molecular motion.
  • Classical Mechanics organizes laws of motion under Newton’s postulates.
  • Electrodynamics unifies electric and magnetic laws.
  • Quantum Theory organizes atomic-scale phenomena.
A theory is not a stronger law. It is an explanatory structure that connects and derives laws. But its domain is larger than the domain of a law. It explains multiple laws of that domain.

3. Sub-Branches of Physics

Physics is organized into sub-branches based on the theoretical frameworks used.

  • Mechanics — motion and forces
  • Thermodynamics — heat and energy transformations
  • Waves and Acoustics — oscillations and wave propagation
  • Electrodynamics — electric and magnetic phenomena
  • Optics — behavior of light
  • Modern Physics — dual nature of light, atoms, nuclei, semiconductors

In addition, there are foundational chapters (General Physics) common to all sub-branches:

  • Physical World
  • Units and Dimensions
  • Measurement and Errors
  • Mathematical Tools
Sub-branches differ in subject matter, but they share common structural principles.

4. Conservation Principles Across Branches

Certain principles are not confined to one branch. They apply universally across physics.

  • Conservation of Energy
  • Conservation of Momentum
  • Conservation of Angular Momentum
  • Conservation of Charge

These conservation principles express structural invariances of nature. They allow us to analyze systems even when full dynamical details are unknown.

For example, in collision problems we may not know the exact forces during impact, yet conservation of momentum allows prediction of outcomes.

Conservation laws act as structural constraints that remain valid across different domains of physics.

5. Architectural View of Physics

Physics can therefore be viewed structurally:

  • Experiments reveal patterns
  • Patterns are expressed as laws
  • Laws are organized within theories
  • Theories define sub-branches
  • Conservation principles constrain all branches
Physics is not a list of formulas. It is a structured architecture of laws, theories, and universal principles.
