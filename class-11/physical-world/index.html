The Physical World

What is the Physical World?

The Physical World is the one, objective universe that we all share and experience. It is the collection of all things that exist in space and time, independent of our individual thoughts or imaginations.

When we observe an apple falling from a tree, the apple, the tree, and the effect of gravity are real, observable facts for everyone. This shared, objective reality is what we seek to understand. The study of science is concerned only with this real, physical world.

Human Curiosity

Direct experiences sparked a persistent drive to ask fundamental questions, often in this order:

  • Why do things happen the way they do? (The initial wonder)
  • How does it work? (The deeper inquiry into the mechanism)
  • What is the world made of? (The fundamental search for constituents)

Science: The Method and The Knowledge

The word "science" refers to two distinct but related concepts:

  1. Science as a Methodology: A self-correcting process for investigating phenomena. All ideas must be rigorously tested against systematic observation and experimental (empirical) evidence.
  2. Science as a Body of Knowledge: The organized, comprehensive, and verifiable body of facts, theories, and laws accumulated over centuries.
Insight: Theory vs. Law
A theory is a robust, well-substantiated explanation for a broad set of phenomena. A physical law is typically a concise, mathematical statement that describes a pattern, such as: $$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$ A law describes what happens, while a theory explains why.

The Structure of the Physical World

Our understanding has evolved from qualitative to quantitative models:

  • The Aristotelian View: A qualitative structure composed of five elements (Earth, Water, Air, Fire, Ether). It was non-mathematical and teleological.
  • The Classical Physics View: A mechanistic and deterministic model.
    • The Stage (Space and Time): Absolute, independent backdrops.
    • The Actors (Matter and Energy): Matter (mass/volume) and Energy (capacity to do work).
    • The Plot (Events): When measurable properties (position, velocity) change in time, an event occurs.

Introduction to Physics

Physics is a fundamental branch of natural science concerned with understanding the natural world and the laws that govern it. It is the discipline that studies the classical components of the physical world: matter, energy, space, and time, and their interactions.

Insight: Formal vs. Natural Science

While Chemistry and Biology are also natural sciences, Mathematics and Logic are formal sciences. Natural sciences (like Physics) are based on empirical evidence and observation of the real world. Formal sciences are based on abstract rules and logical deduction.

Physics uses mathematics as its language, but a mathematical equation is only "true" in physics if it matches an experimental result in the real world.

A Core Definition

"Physics is a scientific study of physical quantities and their mathematical relations, as established in the laws of nature."
  • Scientific study: Relates to a rigorous, systematic process of measurement rather than guesswork.
  • Physical quantities: Measurable properties of the world (length, mass, force).
  • Mathematical relations: Using math to describe real processes (e.g., adding 2kg of mass to 3kg to get 5kg).
  • Laws of nature: Compact mathematical summaries like Coulomb's Law: $$F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$
Physics vs. Physical Chemistry

In Physics (Mechanics), we often assume ideal mixing (mass and volume are conserved):

$$\rho_{\text{mixture}} = \frac{m_1 + m_2}{V_1 + V_2}$$

A Chemist, however, considers intermolecular forces. If forces are stronger, volume decreases and density becomes greater than the ideal calculation. Physics isn't "wrong"; it simply uses a model that is an excellent approximation for many macroscopic situations.

Core Objectives

To manage the infinite complexity of nature, Physics follows two main approaches:

  • Unification: Explaining diverse phenomena with a few universal laws (e.g., Gravity explaining both an apple falling and planetary orbits).
  • Reductionism: Understanding a complex system by breaking it down into parts (e.g., understanding gas pressure by studying individual molecule motion).
Insight: Law vs. Theory

A Physical Law is a quantitative model. It is a compact mathematical summary of "what" happens (e.g., Newton's First Law: if $F=0$, then $a=0$).

A Physical Theory is the broader qualitative framework. It explains "why" a phenomenon occurs and establishes causal relations (e.g., the Kinetic Theory of Gases explains why gas laws work).

The Role of Measurement

Measurement is the foundation of all physical laws. To study the world quantitatively, we require a standardized system of physical quantities and units, which is the focus of our next chapter.

The Primary Coordinates of Existence

If something exists in the real physical world, then two questions must necessarily be meaningful and answerable:

"Where does it exist?"     and     "When does it exist?"

The answer to "where" specifies the position of the object in space, while the answer to "when" specifies the instant of time at which that position is defined. Together, space and time provide the minimum information required to locate any physical entity within the universe.

Insight: Primary vs. Secondary Inquiries

All other questions about a physical system—such as "What is it?", "How does it behave?", or "Why does it behave the way it does?"—are secondary.

These questions can only be meaningfully addressed after the object has been located in space and time. Without a well-defined "where" and "when," no physical description, measurement, or mathematical relation is possible.

Phenomenon vs. Causation: The Pursuit of Truth

In our daily lives, we often describe the world based on how it appears to our senses. These are called phenomena. However, Physics is not merely a record of these appearances; it is a rigorous pursuit of the underlying causation—the objective truth behind the phenomenon.

The Goal of Physics: To peel away the layer of "apparent reality" to discover the "mechanistic reality" that governs the universe.

Case Studies in Physical Truth

1. Mass vs. Weight

The Phenomenon: We step on a scale and say, "My weight is 60 kg."

The Physical Truth: In physics, "60 kg" is your mass (\(m)\), an intrinsic property of matter. Weight is actually a force (\(W)\) caused by the gravitational pull of the Earth.

Relation: $$W = m \cdot g$$

Weight is the causal result of mass interacting with a gravitational field.

2. The Sunrise

The Phenomenon: Every morning, we observe the "Sun rising in the East" and moving across the sky.

The Physical Truth: The Sun is relatively stationary within the solar system. The motion we observe is an apparent motion caused by the Earth’s rotation on its axis.

The "rise" is a phenomenon of the observer's Frame of Reference; the rotation is the physical cause.

Note: As a student of physics, you must train your mind to look past the "common sense" description. When you see an event, your first question should not be "What does it look like?" but "What is the fundamental law causing this interaction?"
