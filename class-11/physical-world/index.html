The Physical World

What is the Physical World?

The Physical World is the one, objective universe that we all share and experience. It is the collection of all things that exist in space and time, independent of our individual thoughts or imaginations.

When we observe an apple falling from a tree, the apple, the tree, and the effect of gravity are real, observable facts for everyone. This shared, objective reality is what we seek to understand. The study of science is concerned only with this real, physical world.

Human Curiosity

Direct experiences sparked a persistent drive to ask fundamental questions, often in this order:

  • Why do things happen the way they do? (The initial wonder)
  • How does it work? (The deeper inquiry into the mechanism)
  • What is the world made of? (The fundamental search for constituents)

Science: The Method and The Knowledge

The word "science" refers to two distinct but related concepts:

  1. Science as a Methodology: A self-correcting process for investigating phenomena. All ideas must be rigorously tested against systematic observation and experimental (empirical) evidence.
  2. Science as a Body of Knowledge: The organized, comprehensive, and verifiable body of facts, theories, and laws accumulated over centuries.
Insight: Theory vs. Law
A theory is a robust, well-substantiated explanation for a broad set of phenomena. A physical law is typically a concise, mathematical statement that describes a pattern, such as: $$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$ A law describes what happens, while a theory explains why.

The Structure of the Physical World

Our understanding has evolved from qualitative to quantitative models:

  • The Aristotelian View: A qualitative structure composed of five elements (Earth, Water, Air, Fire, Ether). It was non-mathematical and teleological.
  • The Classical Physics View: A mechanistic and deterministic model.
    • The Stage (Space and Time): Absolute, independent backdrops.
    • The Actors (Matter and Energy): Matter (mass/volume) and Energy (capacity to do work).
    • The Plot (Events): When measurable properties (position, velocity) change in time, an event occurs.
