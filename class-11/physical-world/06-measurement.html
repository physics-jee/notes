Measurement and Physical Quantities

1. From Phenomenon to Quantity

In everyday language, natural phenomena are described qualitatively.

It rains. It is humid. It is hot. The object moves fast.

Such descriptions are insufficient for scientific study.

Science converts phenomena into well-defined measurable quantities.

A phenomenon becomes scientific only when it is defined quantitatively.

2. Defining Natural Phenomena

Consider rainfall.

In science, rainfall is defined as the volume (or mass) of water falling per unit area per unit time.

Humidity is defined as the ratio of actual vapor pressure to saturation vapor pressure.

Speed is defined as distance travelled per unit time.

These definitions remove vagueness. They specify exactly what is being measured.

Once defined, different observers can measure the same quantity under identical conditions.

3. Physical Quantities

A physical quantity is a measurable property defined with precision.

Every physical quantity consists of:

Numerical value × Unit

Example:

\( 5 \, \text{m} \)

The number gives magnitude. The unit gives meaning.

Without units, numbers have no physical interpretation.

4. Standardization and Reproducibility

For reproducibility, units must be standardized.

A meter must mean the same thing everywhere. A second must be universally defined.

This standardization ensures that measurements performed in different laboratories are comparable.

Science depends on this universality.

5. Mathematical Description

Once quantities are defined and measurable, relations between them can be expressed mathematically.

For example:

\( v = \frac{d}{t} \)

Here velocity is not a vague idea. It is a precisely defined ratio.

Mathematics does not create physical reality. It expresses relations between well-defined quantities.

Definition → Measurement → Mathematical Relation. This is the quantitative structure of physics.

6. Uncertainty and Approximation

No measurement is exact. Every instrument has limitations.

Physics acknowledges this through error analysis and controlled approximation.

Precision is not assumed. It is evaluated.
