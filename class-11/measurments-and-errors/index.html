Measurement & Errors

How close our numbers are to physical reality.

1. Measurement

Measurement is the process of comparing an unknown physical quantity with a known standard.

Two essentials:
  • Measuring instrument
  • Reference unit
Key point: No measurement is exact — every result is an approximation.
A measurement without uncertainty is physically meaningless.

2. Accuracy & Precision

These terms are related but not interchangeable.

Accuracy
  • Closeness to true value
  • Affected by systematic errors
Precision
  • Repeatability of measurements
  • Affected by random errors
A measurement can be precise but inaccurate — and that distinction matters in experiments.

3. Errors

An error is the deviation of a measured value from the true value.

Types of Errors

  • Systematic Errors: Due to faulty calibration or method
  • Random Errors: Due to uncontrollable fluctuations
  • Gross Errors: Human mistakes (ignored in analysis)

4. Absolute, Relative & Percentage Error

Absolute Error: \[ \Delta x = |x_{\text{measured}} - x_{\text{true}}| \]
Relative Error: \[ \frac{\Delta x}{x} \]
Percentage Error: \[ \frac{\Delta x}{x} \times 100\% \]

5. Propagation of Errors

When quantities combine mathematically, their errors propagate in a predictable manner.

  • Addition / Subtraction → absolute errors add
  • Multiplication / Division → relative errors add
  • Powers → relative error multiplied by exponent
If \( Q = x^a y^b \), then \[ \frac{\Delta Q}{Q} = a\frac{\Delta x}{x} + b\frac{\Delta y}{y} \]
Error analysis is bookkeeping of uncertainty, not a statistical luxury.

6. Significant Figures

Significant figures convey the reliability of a measured value.

  • All non-zero digits are significant
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant
  • Trailing zeros are significant only if decimal is present
Significant figures reflect *measurement capability*, not mathematical convenience.
