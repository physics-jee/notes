Laws of Motion

How forces govern changes in motion.

1. Force & Inertia

A force is an external interaction capable of changing the state of motion of a body.

  • Change in speed
  • Change in direction
  • Change in shape
Motion does not require force. Change of motion does.

2. Newton’s First Law

A body continues in its state of rest or uniform straight-line motion unless acted upon by a net external force.

Net external force = 0 ⇒ acceleration = 0

This law defines inertial frames of reference.

The first law is not a special case of the second — it defines the condition under which the second law applies.

3. Newton’s Second Law

The rate of change of momentum of a body is proportional to the net external force acting on it and occurs in the direction of that force.

\[ \vec F = \frac{d\vec p}{dt} \]

For constant mass:

\[ \vec F = m\vec a \]
Newton’s second law is the *equation of motion* of classical mechanics.

4. Newton’s Third Law

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

\[ \vec F_{AB} = -\vec F_{BA} \]
  • Action–reaction forces act on different bodies
  • They never cancel each other
The third law explains interaction, not equilibrium.

5. Mass & Inertia

Mass is a measure of a body's resistance to change in motion.

  • Inertial mass → resistance to acceleration
  • Gravitational mass → interaction with gravity
In classical mechanics, inertial and gravitational mass are equivalent.

6. Free Body Diagrams

A free body diagram (FBD) shows all external forces acting on a body.

  • Choose the body clearly
  • Show only forces acting *on* the body
  • Resolve forces along convenient axes
Most mistakes in dynamics are diagrammatic, not algebraic.
