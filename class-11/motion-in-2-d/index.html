Motion in Two Dimensions

Motion where direction matters as much as magnitude.

1. Position Vector & Plane Motion

When motion is not restricted to a straight line, we describe position using vectors in a plane.

\[ \vec r = x\,\hat i + y\,\hat j \]
Motion in 2D is not “more physics” — it is the same physics, expressed vectorially.

2. Displacement, Velocity & Acceleration

All kinematic quantities in 2D are vectors.

\[ \vec v = \frac{d\vec r}{dt}, \qquad \vec a = \frac{d\vec v}{dt} \]
  • Velocity is always tangent to the path
  • Acceleration need not be along velocity

3. Component Resolution

Any vector in a plane can be resolved into perpendicular components.

\[ \vec v = v_x \hat i + v_y \hat j \]
\( v_x = v \cos\theta \)
\( v_y = v \sin\theta \)
In kinematics, components evolve independently as long as acceleration components are independent.

4. Projectile Motion

Projectile motion is a special case of 2D motion under uniform gravity.

  • Horizontal motion → constant velocity
  • Vertical motion → uniformly accelerated
Time of flight: \[ T = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g} \] Range: \[ R = \frac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g} \]
Projectile motion is simply two independent 1D motions sharing time.

5. Relative Motion in Two Dimensions

Motion is often analyzed relative to another moving object.

\[ \vec v_{A/B} = \vec v_A - \vec v_B \]
  • Essential for rain–man, river–boat, and chase problems
  • Reference frame choice simplifies analysis

6. Trajectory

The trajectory is the path traced by a particle during motion.

For projectile motion: \[ y = x\tan\theta - \frac{gx^2}{2u^2\cos^2\theta} \]
Trajectory is a geometric consequence of time-dependent motion.
