Units & Dimensions

The grammar that ensures physical equations make sense.

1. Physical Quantities

A physical quantity is something that can be measured and expressed using a number and a reference standard.

Two Components:
  • Numerical magnitude
  • Unit (reference standard)
Example: Velocity = 5 m/s
5 → magnitude, m/s → unit
Physics does not deal with pure numbers. Every meaningful number must be tied to a physical reference.

2. Units and Systems of Units

A unit is a chosen standard used to measure a physical quantity.

Fundamental Units (SI)

  • Length → metre (m)
  • Mass → kilogram (kg)
  • Time → second (s)
  • Electric current → ampere (A)
  • Temperature → kelvin (K)
  • Amount of substance → mole (mol)
  • Luminous intensity → candela (cd)

Derived Units

Obtained by combining fundamental units.

Force = mass × acceleration
\( \Rightarrow [F] = \text{kg·m·s}^{-2} \)

3. Dimensions

Dimensions express the dependence of a physical quantity on fundamental quantities.

\[ [Q] = M^a L^b T^c \]
  • Dimensions are independent of units
  • Same dimensions ≠ same physical quantity
Energy and torque have identical dimensions, but represent entirely different physical ideas.

4. Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is a logical consistency check, not a derivation tool.

Applications

  • Checking correctness of equations
  • Deriving relations up to dimensionless constants
  • Converting between unit systems
If \([LHS] \neq [RHS]\), the equation is physically impossible.

5. Limitations of Dimensional Methods

  • Cannot determine numerical constants (½, 2π, etc.)
  • Fails for trigonometric, exponential, or logarithmic relations
  • Cannot distinguish between quantities with same dimensions
Dimensional analysis checks *syntax*, not the *semantics* of physics.
