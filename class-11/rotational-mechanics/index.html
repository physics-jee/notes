Rotational Mechanics

Dynamics of rigid bodies about a fixed or moving axis.

1. Rigid Body

A rigid body is an ideal system of particles in which the distance between any two particles remains constant.

Rigid body motion = translation of COM + rotation about COM.

2. Angular Variables

Rotational motion is described using angular quantities analogous to linear motion.

Angular displacement: \( \theta \)
Angular velocity: \( \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \)
Angular acceleration: \( \alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt} \)

3. Linear–Angular Relations

\( v = r\omega \)
\( a_t = r\alpha \)
\( a_c = r\omega^2 \)
All particles share the same \( \omega \), but not the same linear velocity.

4. Moment of Inertia

The moment of inertia measures a body's resistance to angular acceleration.

\[ I = \sum m_i r_i^2 \]
  • Depends on mass distribution
  • Depends on choice of axis
Mass resists linear acceleration, moment of inertia resists angular acceleration.

5. Parallel & Perpendicular Axis Theorems

Parallel Axis: \[ I = I_{\text{CM}} + Md^2 \]
Perpendicular Axis (planar bodies): \[ I_z = I_x + I_y \]

6. Torque

Torque is the rotational analogue of force.

\[ \vec \tau = \vec r \times \vec F \]
  • Depends on force and lever arm
  • Direction given by right-hand rule
Large force does not guarantee large torque.

7. Equation of Rotational Motion

\[ \vec \tau_{\text{net}} = I\vec \alpha \]

This is the rotational analogue of Newton’s second law.

8. Angular Momentum

Angular momentum describes rotational state of motion.

Particle: \[ \vec L = \vec r \times \vec p \]

Rigid body (about fixed axis): \[ L = I\omega \]

9. Conservation of Angular Momentum

If net external torque is zero, angular momentum remains constant.

\[ L_{\text{initial}} = L_{\text{final}} \]
Angular momentum conservation explains spinning skaters and collapsing stars.

10. Rolling Motion

Rolling without slipping is a combination of translation and rotation.

\[ v_{\text{CM}} = \omega R \]
  • Point of contact has zero velocity
  • Static friction enables rolling
Rolling is constrained motion, not pure rotation.

11. Common Mistakes

  • Using wrong axis for moment of inertia
  • Forgetting torque of normal / friction
  • Mixing particle and rigid body formulas
