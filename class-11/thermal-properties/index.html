Thermal Properties of Matter

Temperature, measurement, and expansion due to heat.

1. Temperature

Temperature is a physical quantity that determines the direction of heat flow between bodies.

  • Heat flows from higher to lower temperature
  • Temperature is a measure of thermal state, not heat content
Two bodies at the same temperature need not contain the same amount of heat.

2. Thermal Equilibrium & Zeroth Law

If two systems are separately in thermal equilibrium with a third, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

The Zeroth Law gives temperature a measurable meaning.

3. Temperature Scales

Celsius Scale
  • Ice point: 0 °C
  • Steam point: 100 °C
Kelvin Scale
  • Absolute zero: 0 K
  • No negative temperatures
\[ T(\text{K}) = t(^{\circ}\text{C}) + 273.15 \]
Kelvin is the fundamental SI scale of temperature.

4. Measurement of Temperature

Temperature is measured using thermometric properties that change uniformly with temperature.

  • Volume of liquid (mercury, alcohol)
  • Electrical resistance
  • Thermoelectric emf
A thermometer does not measure heat — it measures a property correlated with temperature.

5. Thermal Expansion

Thermal expansion is the increase in dimensions of a body due to rise in temperature.

Expansion occurs because average intermolecular spacing increases.

6. Linear Expansion

\[ \Delta L = \alpha L \Delta T \]
  • \(\alpha\): coefficient of linear expansion
  • Valid for small temperature changes

7. Area & Volume Expansion

Area expansion: \[ \Delta A = 2\alpha A \Delta T \]
Volume expansion: \[ \Delta V = 3\alpha V \Delta T \]
Expansion coefficients are related, not independent.

8. Anomalous Expansion of Water

Water contracts when heated from 0 °C to 4 °C, and expands above 4 °C.

  • Maximum density at 4 °C
  • Responsible for survival of aquatic life
Anomalous expansion is a molecular effect, not a violation of thermal laws.

9. Thermal Stress

If expansion is prevented, thermal stress develops.

\[ \text{Stress} = Y \alpha \Delta T \]
Thermal stress links elasticity with thermal physics.

10. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing heat with temperature
  • Using Celsius in thermodynamic formulas
  • Forgetting constraint conditions in expansion
