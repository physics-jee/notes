Circular Motion

Motion with constant speed but continuously changing velocity.

1. What Makes Circular Motion Special?

In circular motion, the magnitude of velocity may remain constant, but its direction changes continuously.

Any change in velocity — magnitude or direction — implies acceleration.

2. Angular Variables

Motion along a circle is conveniently described using angular quantities.

Angular displacement: \( \theta \)
Angular velocity: \( \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \)
Angular acceleration: \( \alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt} \)

3. Linear–Angular Relations

\( v = r\omega \)
\( a_t = r\alpha \)

These relations hold for a particle moving in a circle of radius \(r\).

Angular variables describe *geometry*, linear variables describe *motion*.

4. Centripetal Acceleration

Even at constant speed, a particle in circular motion experiences acceleration directed toward the center of the circle.

\[ a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2 \]
  • Direction: radially inward
  • Perpendicular to instantaneous velocity
Centripetal acceleration changes direction, not speed.

5. Centripetal Force

The required inward force to maintain circular motion is called centripetal force.

\[ F_c = m\frac{v^2}{r} \]
  • Not a new force
  • Provided by tension, friction, gravity, or normal reaction
“Centripetal force” describes a role, not a physical agent.

6. Common Circular Motion Scenarios

  • Stone tied to a string
  • Vehicle on a circular track
  • Vertical circular motion

In each case, identify which real force provides the required centripetal force.

7. Banking of Roads

Roads are banked to reduce dependence on friction during turns.

\[ \tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg} \]
Banking uses geometry to supply centripetal force.

8. Common Mistakes

  • Assuming centripetal force is an extra force
  • Forgetting direction of acceleration
  • Mixing up tangential and radial components
