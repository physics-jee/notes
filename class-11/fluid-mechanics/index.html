Fluid Mechanics

How liquids and gases respond to forces and flow.

1. What Is a Fluid?

A fluid is a substance that cannot sustain shear stress in static equilibrium and flows under applied force.

Fluids resist compression, but not shape change.

2. Density & Pressure

Density: \[ \rho = \frac{m}{V} \]
Pressure: \[ P = \frac{F}{A} \]
  • Pressure is scalar
  • Acts equally in all directions

3. Pressure Variation with Depth

In a static fluid, pressure increases with depth.

\[ P = P_0 + \rho g h \]
Pressure depends on depth, not container shape.

4. Pascal’s Law

Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished in all directions.

\[ \frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2} \]

Basis of hydraulic press and brakes.

5. Buoyancy & Archimedes’ Principle

A body immersed in a fluid experiences an upward force equal to the weight of fluid displaced.

\[ F_B = \rho_{\text{fluid}} V g \]
  • Apparent weight decreases in fluid
  • Floating depends on average density
Buoyant force depends on fluid, not on the object’s material.

6. Fluid Flow & Continuity Equation

For steady, incompressible flow:

\[ A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 \]
  • Smaller area → higher speed
  • Mass flow rate conserved

7. Bernoulli’s Theorem

Energy conservation applied to fluid flow.

\[ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant} \]
  • Valid for steady, non-viscous flow
  • Explains lift, venturimeter, atomizer
Bernoulli is not magic — it is energy conservation.

8. Viscosity

Viscosity is the internal resistance to fluid flow.

\[ F = \eta A \frac{dv}{dy} \]
  • Higher viscosity → slower flow
  • Liquids: viscosity decreases with temperature

9. Stokes’ Law & Terminal Velocity

\[ F = 6\pi\eta r v \]
Terminal velocity: \[ v_t = \frac{2r^2(\rho_s - \rho_f)g}{9\eta} \]
Terminal velocity arises when net force becomes zero.

10. Surface Tension

Surface tension is the tendency of a liquid surface to minimize area.

\[ T = \frac{F}{L} \]
  • Due to molecular cohesion
  • Responsible for capillarity

11. Capillary Rise

\[ h = \frac{2T\cos\theta}{\rho g r} \]
Capillarity is a balance of surface tension and gravity.

12. Common Mistakes

  • Using Bernoulli where viscosity is important
  • Confusing pressure with force
  • Assuming buoyant force depends on depth
