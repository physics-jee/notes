Calorimetry

Heat transfer analyzed through energy conservation.

1. Heat

Heat is energy transferred between bodies due to a temperature difference.

  • Heat flows spontaneously from higher to lower temperature
  • Heat is not a property of a system
Heat is energy in transit, not stored energy.

2. Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of unit mass of a substance by 1 K.

\[ Q = mc\Delta T \]
  • Depends on material
  • Depends on physical state
Large specific heat means large thermal inertia.

3. Principle of Calorimetry

In an isolated system, total heat exchanged is zero.

\[ \sum Q = 0 \]
  • Heat lost = Heat gained
  • Direct consequence of energy conservation
Calorimetry is the First Law applied to temperature change.

4. Change of State & Latent Heat

During phase change, temperature remains constant while heat is absorbed or released.

\[ Q = mL \]
  • \(L_f\): latent heat of fusion
  • \(L_v\): latent heat of vaporization
Latent heat changes molecular configuration, not temperature.

5. Heating Curve

A heating curve shows temperature variation as heat is supplied continuously.

  • Sloping regions → temperature change
  • Flat regions → phase change

6. Calorimetry of Mixtures

When substances at different temperatures are mixed, final temperature is found using heat balance.

\[ \sum m c (T_f - T_i) = 0 \]
Always define a consistent sign convention before writing equations.

7. Calorimeter & Water Equivalent

A calorimeter is a device used to measure heat exchange.

Water equivalent: \[ W = \frac{m_c c_c}{c_w} \]
  • Accounts for heat absorbed by container
  • Simplifies heat balance equations
Ignoring calorimeter heat capacity leads to systematic errors.

8. Common Mistakes

  • Using energy conservation without thermal isolation
  • Mixing Celsius and Kelvin carelessly
  • Forgetting latent heat during phase change
