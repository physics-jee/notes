Series and Parallel Combinations

Structural reduction of linear networks into equivalent black-box parameters.

3.1 The Concept of Equivalent Resistance

The core principle of circuit reduction is replacement without external alteration. If a complex network of resistors is placed inside a "black box" with two emerging terminals, the Equivalent Resistance (\(R_{eq}\)) is the value of a single resistor that could replace the entire box such that for the same applied potential difference (\(V\)), the exact same total current (\(I\)) is drawn from the source.

3.2 Series Combination: The Constraint of Charge Conservation

Resistors are in series if they are connected end-to-end such that there are no branching nodes between them.

The Structural Rule: Because charge cannot accumulate at any point in a steady-state conductor (Conservation of Charge), the exact same current \(I\) must flow through every resistor.

Derivation via Energy Conservation

The total potential drop across the combination must equal the sum of the individual potential drops (Conservation of Energy):

\[V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3\]

Applying Ohm's Law (\(V = IR\)) to each component:

\[I R_{eq} = I R_1 + I R_2 + I R_3\] \[R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3\]
In series, the equivalent resistance is always strictly greater than the largest individual resistance in the chain. The circuit acts as a voltage divider, where \(V \propto R\).

3.3 Parallel Combination: The Constraint of Equipotential Nodes

Resistors are in parallel if all their starting ends are connected to a single common node, and all their terminating ends are connected to another common node.

The Structural Rule: Because the terminals of all resistors share the same two equipotential nodes, the potential difference (\(V\)) across every resistor is strictly identical, regardless of their resistance values.

Derivation via Charge Conservation

The total current entering the primary node must equal the sum of the currents branching into the individual resistors:

\[I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2 + I_3\]

Applying Ohm's Law (\(I = V/R\)) to each branch:

\[\frac{V}{R_{eq}} = \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2} + \frac{V}{R_3}\] \[\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3}\]
In parallel, the equivalent resistance is always strictly less than the smallest individual resistance in the network. The circuit acts as a current divider, where \(I \propto 1/R\).
