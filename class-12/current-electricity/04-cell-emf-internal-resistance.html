Cell EMF and Internal Resistance

The non-electrostatic mechanism that sustains steady-state dynamics.

4.1 The Mechanism of a Cell

An electric cell is not a source of charge; it is an energy pump. In an external circuit, positive charge naturally flows from high potential to low potential along the electric field. To maintain a steady current, there must be a mechanism inside the cell that pumps the charge back from low potential to high potential, against the electrostatic force.

This requires a non-electrostatic force (usually chemical), denoted as \(\vec{F}_n\).

4.2 Electromotive Force (EMF)

Despite its historical name, EMF (\(E\) or \(\varepsilon\)) is not a force. It is defined as the work done by the non-electrostatic agency in moving a unit positive charge from the lower potential terminal to the higher potential terminal.

\[E = \frac{W_{\text{non-electrostatic}}}{q}\]
EMF is an intrinsic characteristic of the cell's chemistry. It represents the maximum possible potential difference across the cell terminals when absolutely no current is drawn (an open circuit).

4.3 Internal Resistance and Terminal Voltage

When charge is pumped through the cell, it must physically travel through the electrolyte. The electrolyte is not a perfect conductor; it collides with the moving ions, dissipating some of the pumped energy. This opposition is the Internal Resistance (\(r\)).

Because of this internal energy loss, the voltage available to the external circuit—the Terminal Voltage (\(V\))—is not always equal to the EMF.

Case 1: Discharging (Acting as a Source)

Current \(I\) leaves the positive terminal. The cell does work, but loses \(Ir\) volts internally.

\[V = E - Ir\]

Case 2: Charging (Acting as a Load)

An external, stronger source forces current \(I\) into the positive terminal. The external source must overcome both the cell's EMF and its internal resistance.

\[V = E + Ir\]
