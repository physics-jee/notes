Electrostatics

The first complete mathematical formulation of the concept of field.

Conceptual Overview

Electrostatics studies electric charges at rest and the physical quantities associated with them — force, electric field, and electric potential. It marks the transition in physics from describing interactions as direct action-at-a-distance to describing them through a field that exists throughout space.

Instead of saying that one charge directly pulls another, electrostatics introduces the idea that a charge modifies the space around it. That modification is the electric field.

Field as the Central Idea

The core concept of electrostatics is the electric field. A field assigns a physical quantity to every point in space. Unlike matter, which is localized, a field is distributed continuously throughout space.

Matter carries intrinsic properties such as mass or charge. A field carries measurable parameters such as electric field strength and potential. Both matter and field can store energy, and both influence the evolution of physical systems.

Electrostatics is therefore not merely the study of charges, but the first systematic mathematical theory of a physical field.

Field Strength and Potential

In mechanics, force is a vector quantity while energy is a scalar quantity. They describe the same physical system from different perspectives: force determines how motion changes locally, while energy provides a global measure of the system.

Electrostatics exhibits a similar dual description. The electric field \( \vec{E} \) is a vector quantity that determines the force on a charge at a point. The electric potential \( V \) is a scalar quantity that encodes the same field information in an integrated form.

\[ \vec{E} = - \nabla V \]

Thus, potential is not a separate physical entity; it is another mathematical representation of the same field.

Visually, \( \vec{E} \) is represented by field lines, while \( V \) is represented by equipotential surfaces. Together, they provide complementary descriptions of the electric field.

Coulomb’s Law and Gauss’ Law

Coulomb’s law and Gauss’ law are two complementary formulations of electrostatics. Both describe the same electric field, but they approach it from different perspectives.

Coulomb’s Law: Field from Source Elements

Coulomb’s law gives the electric field produced by a point charge:

\[ \vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}\,\hat{r} \]

For extended charge distributions, the total field is obtained using superposition:

\[ \vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \int \frac{dq}{r^2}\,\hat{r} \]

Thus, Coulomb’s law builds the field by summing contributions from individual charge elements.

Gauss’ Law: Field from Flux

Gauss’ law relates the electric flux through a closed surface to the total charge enclosed:

\[ \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{S} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0} \]

It does not directly give the field at a point. Instead, it provides a global constraint on how the field flows through space. When strong symmetry is present, this constraint allows direct determination of \( \vec{E} \).

Relationship Between the Two

  • Coulomb’s law constructs the field locally from charge elements.
  • Gauss’ law describes how the field behaves globally.
  • In fact, Gauss’ law can be derived from Coulomb’s law using calculus.
  • Both are consistent descriptions of the same physical field.

Coulomb’s law emphasizes the inverse-square nature of interaction. Gauss’ law emphasizes the geometric structure of the field.

Organisation of Field and Potential

Electrostatics develops in two distinct structural layers:

I. Direct Integration (Coulomb World)

We begin with Coulomb’s law and the principle of superposition. Electric field and potential are obtained by integrating contributions from infinitesimal charge elements \( dq \).

This method is used for:

  • Point charge and discrete systems of charges
  • Finite straight rod
  • Circular ring (field on axis, zero field at center)
  • Arc (field at center)
  • Long straight wire
  • Circular disc (axis)
  • Annular disc (superposition of discs)
  • Infinite sheet
  • Spherical cap (at center)

Here, symmetry helps reduce components, but integration is essential.

II. Symmetry-Based Method (Gauss World)

When charge distributions possess strong symmetry (planar, cylindrical, or spherical), Gauss' law allows direct determination of the electric field without performing explicit integration.

This becomes especially powerful for volume charge densities:

  • Uniformly charged solid sphere
  • Non-uniform spherical distributions \( \rho(r) \)
  • Spherical shell
  • Infinite cylinder (solid and hollow)
  • Infinite thick sheet

Gauss’ law does not replace Coulomb’s law. It accelerates computation when symmetry determines the structure of \( \vec{E} \).

Structural Insight

  • Coulomb’s law builds the field from pieces.
  • Gauss’ law reveals how symmetry organises the field globally.
  • Both describe the same physics.

Core Topics

  • Coulomb’s Law — Inverse-square interaction between point charges; foundation of electrostatic force.
  • Electric Field — Definition, superposition principle, and visualization through field lines.
  • Electric Potential — Scalar description of the field and its relation to energy.
  • Equipotential Surfaces — Geometric representation of constant potential and their relation to \( \vec{E} \).
  • Gauss’ Law — Symmetry-based method for determining electric fields.
