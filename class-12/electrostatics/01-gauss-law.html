Application of Gauss Law (AGL)

Gauss Law is fundamental on its own but needs symmetry for applicability

Electric Field of a Uniformly Charged Spherical Shell

Consider a spherical shell of radius R carrying total charge Q uniformly distributed over its surface.

Step 1: Symmetry of the Charge Distribution

The charge distribution is spherically symmetric. This means:

  • The system looks identical under any rotation about the center.
  • No direction in space is preferred.
  • All points at the same distance \( r \) from the center are physically equivalent.

Because the laws of electrostatics respect spatial symmetry, the electric field must inherit this symmetry.

Step 2: What Symmetry Forces on \( \vec{E} \)

From spherical symmetry, we conclude:

  • \( \vec{E} \) cannot have any tangential component.
  • \( \vec{E} \) must be radial.
  • The magnitude \( |\vec{E}| \) can depend only on the distance \( r \), not on angular position.

Therefore,

\[ \vec{E}(r) = E(r)\,\hat{r} \]

This conclusion is obtained before applying Gauss Law and both steps combined together are called as symmetry argument due spherical distribution. It will be used further for all spherical cases and is very strong tool.

Step 3: Application of Gauss Law

Now choose a spherical Gaussian surface of radius \( r \). Since \( |\vec{E}| \) is constant over this surface and \( \vec{E} \) is parallel to \( d\vec{S} \), the surface integral simplifies:

\[ \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{S} = E(r) \oint dS = E(r)\,(4\pi r^2) \]

This simplification is possible only because of the symmetry established earlier.

\[ E(r)\,(4\pi r^2) = \frac{Q_{\text{enclosed}}}{\varepsilon_0} \]

Case I: \( r \lt R \)

\[ Q_{\text{enclosed}} = 0 \] \[ E(r) = 0 \]

Case II: \( r \gt R \)

\[ Q_{\text{enclosed}} = Q \] \[ E(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2} \] \[ \vec{E}(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2}\,\hat{r} \]

Field at \( r = R \)

  • \( \lim_{r \to R^-} E = 0 \)
  • \( \lim_{r \to R^+} \displaystyle E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^2} \)

The electric field is discontinuous at the surface \( r = R \). The limiting values from inside and outside are different. Therefore, the field at exactly \( r = R \) is not uniquely defined.

Structural Insight

Gauss Law alone gives:

\[ \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{S} = \frac{Q_{\text{enclosed}}}{\varepsilon_0} \]

But without symmetry, the integral cannot be simplified. Symmetry determines the form of \( \vec{E} \). Gauss Law then determines its magnitude.

Electric Potential of a Uniformly Charged Spherical Shell

(a) For \( r \ge R \)

We use the concept that outside the sphere, the spherical charge distribution behaves like a point charge at the center. So, the formula for potential is same as that of a point charge. This is true only for outside points.

\[ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r} \]

(b) For \( r \le R \)

Since the electric field inside is zero, potential remains constant:

\[ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R} \]

Continuity at \( r = R \)

\[ V(R^-) = V(R^+) = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R} \]

The potential is continuous at the surface, but its derivative changes abruptly. Since

\[ \vec{E} = -\nabla V \]

the discontinuity in the derivative of potential produces the jump in the electric field.
