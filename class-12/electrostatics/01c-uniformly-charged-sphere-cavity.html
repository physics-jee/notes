Electric Field inside Cavity of Uniformly Charged Sphere

Using superposition principle

Problem Setup

A non-conducting solid sphere of radius R has uniform volume charge density \( \rho \). A spherical cavity of radius r is carved out, whose center is displaced from the center of the original sphere by vector \( \vec{a} \).

Find the electric field at any point inside the cavity.

Geometric Setup for Superposition

Step 1: Use Superposition

The configuration can be treated as:

  • A complete uniformly charged sphere of density \( \rho \)
  • Plus a smaller sphere of density \( -\rho \) placed at the cavity center

(The cavity is equivalent to adding negative charge density.)

Step 2: Field due to a Uniformly Charged Solid Sphere

Inside a uniformly charged solid sphere,

\[ \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{3\varepsilon_0} \vec{r} \]

where \( \vec{r} \) is position vector from the center of that sphere.

Step 3: Field at Point P inside Cavity

Let:

  • \( \vec{r}_1 \) = position vector of point P from center of big sphere
  • \( \vec{r}_2 \) = position vector of point P from center of cavity
  • \( \vec{a} \) = displacement vector from big sphere center to cavity center
Then geometrically, \[ \vec{r}_1 = \vec{a} + \vec{r}_2 \]

Step 4: Apply Superposition

Field due to full sphere: \[ \vec{E}_1 = \frac{\rho}{3\varepsilon_0} \vec{r}_1 \] Field due to negative sphere: \[ \vec{E}_2 = \frac{\rho}{3\varepsilon_0} \vec{r}_2 \] Net field inside cavity: \[ \vec{E} = \vec{E}_1 - \vec{E}_2 \] \[ \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{3\varepsilon_0} (\vec{r}_1 - \vec{r}_2) \] Substitute \( \vec{r}_1 = \vec{a} + \vec{r}_2 \): \[ \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{3\varepsilon_0} \vec{a} \]

Final Result

\[ \boxed{ \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{3\varepsilon_0} \vec{a} } \]

The electric field inside the cavity is:

  • Uniform (same magnitude and direction at every point inside the cavity).
  • Directed along \( \vec{a} \) (from the center of the original sphere to the center of the cavity).
  • Independent of the position of point \( P \) inside the cavity.
  • Dependent only on the displacement vector \( \vec{a} \), and independent of the cavity radius.
  • If the cavity is concentric (\( \vec{a} = 0 \)), then \( \vec{E} = 0 \).

Structural Insight

  • The linear dependence \( \vec{E} \propto \vec{r} \) inside a uniform sphere is the key.
  • Subtracting two linear fields produces a constant field.
  • This result holds only because density is uniform.
  • For non-uniform \( \rho(r) \), field in cavity is not uniform.
