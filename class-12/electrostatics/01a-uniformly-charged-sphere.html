E, V for Uniformly Charged Sphere

Using Shell values to calculate for Sphere

Electric Field of a Uniformly Charged Solid Sphere

Consider a solid sphere of radius R with total charge Q uniformly distributed throughout its volume.

Step 1: Symmetry Argument

The charge distribution is spherically symmetric. Therefore, by the same symmetry reasoning used for the spherical shell:

  • \( \vec{E} \) must be radial.
  • The magnitude \( E \) depends only on distance \( r \) from the center.
\[ \vec{E}(r) = E(r)\,\hat{r} \]

Step 2: Charge Density

Since charge is uniformly distributed in volume,

\[ \rho = \frac{Q}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3} \]

Case I: \( r \lt R \) (Inside the Sphere)

Choose a spherical Gaussian surface of radius \( r \). The enclosed charge is proportional to enclosed volume:

\[ Q_{\text{enclosed}} = \rho \left( \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \right) \] Substitute \( \rho \): \[ Q_{\text{enclosed}} = Q \frac{r^3}{R^3} \] Now apply Gauss Law: \[ E(r)(4\pi r^2) = \frac{Q r^3}{\varepsilon_0 R^3} \] \[ E(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^3} r \] \[ \vec{E}(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^3} r\,\hat{r} \]

Inside the sphere, the electric field increases linearly with \( r \).

Case II: \( r > R \) (Outside the Sphere)

The entire charge \( Q \) is enclosed.

\[ E(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2} \] \[ \vec{E}(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2}\,\hat{r} \]

Continuity at \( r = R \)

Inside value at \( r = R \):

\[ E(R^-) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^2} \]

Outside value at \( r = R \):

\[ E(R^+) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^2} \]

Thus, unlike the spherical shell, the electric field is continuous at the surface.

Electric Potential of a Uniformly Charged Solid Sphere

(a) For \( r \ge R \)

We use the concept that outside the sphere, the spherical charge distribution behaves like a point charge at the center. So, the formula for potential is same as that of a point charge. This is true only for outside points.

\[ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r} \]

For \( r = R \) at the surface:

\[ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R} = \frac{kQ}{R} \]

(b) For \( r \lt R \)

To find the potential inside, we use:

\[ V(r) - V(\infty) = - \int_{\infty}^{r} \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} \]

where \(V(\infty) = 0\)

We break the integration into two regions:

\[ V(r) = - \left( \int_{\infty}^{R} E_{\text{outside}}\,dr + \int_{R}^{r} E_{\text{inside}}\,dr \right) \]

We split the integral at \( R \) because the expression for \( E \) changes there. Even if \( E \) were discontinuous at \( R \), the integral would still be valid provided the discontinuity is finite (bounded).

First Integral (Outside Region)

\[ \int_{\infty}^{R} \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2}\,dr = - \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R} \]

Second Integral (Inside Region)

Inside field: \[ E(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^3} r \] Now integrate: \[ \int_{R}^{r} \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^3} r\,dr = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R^3} \left( \frac{r^2}{2} - \frac{R^2}{2} \right) \]

Final Expression

Combining both parts: \[ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R} - \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{2R^3} \left( r^2 - R^2 \right) \] Simplifying: \[ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{Q}{2R^3} \left( 3R^2 - r^2 \right) \]

Continuity at \( r = R \)

\[ V(R^-) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R} = V(R^+) \]

Thus, the potential is continuous at the surface. Unlike the shell case, both \( E \) and \( V \) are continuous.

Structural Insight

  • Inside the solid sphere, \( E \propto r \) (linear).
  • Inside the solid sphere, \( V \propto (3R^2 - r^2) \) (parabola).
  • Outside, both reduce to point charge behavior.
