Electric Field for Non-Uniformly Charged Sphere

Spherically symmetric volume charge density \( \rho = \rho(r) \)

General Spherically Symmetric Charge Distribution

Consider a solid sphere of radius R whose volume charge density depends only on distance from the center:

\[ \rho = \rho(r) \]

Step 1: Symmetry Argument

Since the charge density depends only on \( r \), the distribution is spherically symmetric. Therefore:

  • \( \vec{E} \) must be radial.
  • The magnitude \( E \) depends only on distance \( r \).
\[ \vec{E}(r) = E(r)\,\hat{r} \]

Step 2: Enclosed Charge (General Formula)

To apply Gauss Law, we first compute the charge enclosed within a Gaussian sphere of radius \( r \):

\[ Q_{\text{enc}}(r) = \int_0^r \rho(r') \, dV \] In spherical coordinates: \[ dV = 4\pi r'^2 dr' \] Therefore, \[ Q_{\text{enc}}(r) = 4\pi \int_0^r \rho(r')\, r'^2 \, dr' \]

Step 3: Apply Gauss Law

\[ E(r)(4\pi r^2) = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}(r)}{\varepsilon_0} \] Thus, \[ E(r) = \frac{1}{\varepsilon_0 r^2} \int_0^r \rho(r')\, r'^2 dr' \]

This is the general formula for the electric field inside any spherically symmetric charge distribution.

Special Case: \( \rho(r) = \rho_0 r^n \)

Let the volume charge density vary as a power of \( r \):

\[ \rho(r) = \rho_0 r^n \]

Enclosed Charge

\[ Q_{\text{enc}}(r) = 4\pi \rho_0 \int_0^r r'^{n+2} dr' \] \[ = 4\pi \rho_0 \frac{r^{n+3}}{n+3} \quad (n \neq -3) \]

Electric Field Inside \( (r \lt R) \)

\[ E(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}(r)}{r^2} \] \[ \boxed{ E(r) = \frac{\rho_0}{\varepsilon_0}\frac{r^{\,n+1}}{n+3} } \] \[ \vec{E}(r) = \frac{\rho_0}{\varepsilon_0}\frac{r^{\,n+1}}{n+3}\,\hat{r} \]

Electric Field Outside \( (r > R) \)

Outside the sphere, only total charge matters:

\[ Q = 4\pi \rho_0 \frac{R^{n+3}}{n+3} \] \[ \vec{E}(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2}\,\hat{r} \]

Thus, outside the sphere behaves like a point charge at the center, irrespective of how charge is distributed inside.

Structural Insight

  • If \( \rho(r) \propto r^n \), then \( E(r) \propto r^{n+1} \).
  • Uniform case (\( n = 0 \)) gives \( E \propto r \).
  • Outside field is always \( \propto 1/r^2 \).
  • Symmetry determines direction; Gauss Law determines magnitude.
